\section{Diagnostic Analysis}

\subsection{Retrieval Is the Bottleneck}
We sampled 20 FAISS failures (best system) and classified them by retrieval context length.
All 20 had short or empty retrieval context ($<$200 characters), compared to ${\sim}500$ chars average for correct answers.
Heuristic classification estimates ${\sim}55\%$ retrieval failure vs.\ ${\sim}45\%$ reasoning failure, suggesting that improving retrieval quality---particularly temporal-aware retrieval---would yield larger gains than improving reasoning capability.

\subsection{Confidence Is Anti-Calibrated}
A striking diagnostic finding: vector-based systems produce \emph{anti-calibrated} confidence scores.
Table~\ref{tab:calibration} shows Expected Calibration Error (ECE), Brier score, and AUROC for each system.

\begin{table}[t]
\centering
\small
\begin{tabular}{lccc}
\toprule
\textbf{System} & \textbf{ECE}$\downarrow$ & \textbf{Brier}$\downarrow$ & \textbf{AUROC} \\
\midrule
FAISS & 0.163 & 0.229 & 0.453 \\
Naive RAG & 0.319 & 0.303 & 0.552 \\
BM25 & 0.235 & 0.769 & 0.467 \\
Simple KG & 0.672 & 0.672 & 0.500 \\
ChromaDB & 0.412 & 0.412 & 0.500 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\caption{Confidence calibration metrics. FAISS and BM25 show AUROC${}<0.5$---wrong answers receive \emph{higher} confidence than correct ones.}
\label{tab:calibration}
\end{table}

FAISS's AUROC of 0.453 means confidence scores are \emph{worse than random} at predicting correctness: wrong answers receive higher confidence than correct ones.
This has practical implications: agents cannot rely on internal confidence to decide whether to trust a temporal memory answer or seek verification.

\subsection{Knowledge Retraction vs.\ Version Tracking}
We decompose performance on questions involving knowledge dynamics (Table~\ref{tab:retraction}).
Simple KG shows an asymmetric profile: strong at version tracking (81.0\%, close to FAISS's 82.8\%) but weak at knowledge retraction (53.6\%).
This suggests graph structures naturally support tracking fact evolution but struggle with explicitly revoking superseded knowledge---a distinction with implications for memory architecture design.

\subsection{Implications for Memory System Design}
Our findings suggest three design recommendations:

\paragraph{1. Temporal-aware retrieval.}
Since ${\sim}55\%$ of failures are retrieval-limited, adding temporal metadata filtering (e.g., ``retrieve only facts recorded before $T$'') or bitemporal indexing would likely yield larger gains than improving the reasoning model.

\paragraph{2. Hybrid vector+graph architectures.}
The complementarity between systems (oracle +9.8pp) and Simple KG's unique adversarial robustness suggest combining dense retrieval's breadth with graph structure's precision would outperform either alone.

\paragraph{3. External verification for temporal claims.}
Anti-calibrated confidence means internal signals cannot identify uncertain temporal answers. External verification (e.g., cross-checking against source timestamps) should be triggered for all bitemporal queries.
